2. What is an appropriate set of hypotheses for this task? What kind of statistical test do you expect to perform? Justify your choices.
H0 : μC = μI // population mean of congruent condition = population mean of incongruent condition
HA : μC ≠ μI // population mean of congruent condition does not equal population mean of incongruent condition

Since we are trying to figure out whether or not they are equal, this is a sensible set of hypotheses to start out with. If we had a prior belief that one was faster than the other, we could set what we were trying to prove(ie μc < μi ) as the alternative hypothesis and set the null hypothesis as the opposite (ie μC >= μI). In this case, since we are not entering with a prior belief and we are simply checking whether these two conditions are different, this set of hypotheses is appropriate.

I will perform a dependent t-test for paired samples. Since this experiment uses a repeated measures design, where each participant has multiple measures, a dependent t-test is appropriate. Since we are testing for a change in either direction between the two samples, the situation calls for a two-tailed test. We can use an alpha of 0.05.

5. Now, perform the statistical test and report your results. What is your confidence level and your critical statistic value? Do you reject the null hypothesis or fail to reject it? Come to a conclusion in terms of the experiment task. Did the results match up with your expectations?

t = (x̄C - x̄I) / (s/√n) -8.021
confidence interval = mean difference ± tCRITICAL (s / √n) = (-10.020,-5.910)
tCRITICAL values = +/- 2.069 for α = 0.05 and df = 23

The t statistic is greater than the absolute value of the tcritical value. This means that it falls inside the critical region, and we can safely reject the null hypothesis. We conclude that the congruent condition is significantly faster than the incongruent condition. The results did line up with expectations, because it was much easier (and faster) to do the congruent test than the incongruent test.

While I completed this analysis for α = .05, the size of the t statistic means we can confidently say μC ≠ μI even with p < .001 .
